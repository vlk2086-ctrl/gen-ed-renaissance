The SCAD General Education Renaissance: Preparing for the Creative Careers of the Future

This toolkit gives SCAD's academic leaders a transparent, shareable view into the General Education Renaissance — the research, the process, and the conversations shaping how we prepare students for AI-augmented creative careers.

Explore the project
Why This Project Exists

The problem we're studying

As AI performs more of the cognitive tasks general education has historically taught, SCAD is asking a foundational question: are we still prioritizing the right competencies?

The Signal

A shifting labor market

Industry research indicates a large share of jobs face AI-driven disruption, and that a meaningful portion of required skills turn over within just a few years — while many recent graduates report being unemployed or underemployed within a year of finishing school.

The Stakes

We train for a career, not a first job

If students are being prepared for four to five decades of work, the competencies worth building are the ones that compound over time: judgment, discernment, adaptability, and the capacity to ask questions that haven't been asked yet.

The Barrier

Uncertainty is real — and worth naming

Faculty and alumni understandably carry AI-related anxiety. This project treats that anxiety as data, not an obstacle to route around, and builds curriculum change on evidence rather than assumption.

What We're Doing About It

A multi-stakeholder, evidence-first approach

A phased research study gathers perspectives from employers, alumni, academic leaders, and faculty before any curriculum changes are proposed — so decisions are grounded in what people actually need, not assumptions about it.

What the research asks

Where do employers, alumni, and faculty agree — and diverge — on the competencies that matter most? Do SCAD's current general education outcomes still hold up? What's actually feasible to teach and assess?

Why this toolkit exists

Academic leaders are on the front line of these conversations with faculty. This site gives you the source material — the research, the literature, the standards — plus spaces to think out loud together before decisions are finalized.

renaissance

/ˈren.ə.sɑːns/ — REN-uh-sahnss

A moment when people consciously reawaken curiosity, creativity, learning, and ambition — and choose to rebuild culture around human possibility.

Think of it less as a time period and more as a pattern that can happen anywhere.

1. Renewal after stagnation Happens when people feel something has gone stale and actively seek fresh thinking.
2. Return to fundamentals Rediscovering core principles or forgotten wisdom, then reinterpreting them for a new moment.
3. Explosion of curiosity Values questioning, experimentation, and cross-disciplinary thinking — a refusal to accept limits.
4. Human-centered progress Assumes people are capable of growth, invention, and transformation — individually and collectively.
5. Cultural momentum When enough people adopt the mindset, it becomes contagious. Innovation accelerates.
Six Competencies

What the data is pointing us toward

Early findings across employers, alumni, academic leaders, and faculty converge around six competencies — chosen by what the data shows matters most, not by department. Each has its own discussion channel below.

1. Critical Thinking

Rated essential across every stakeholder group, and the one skill everyone still chose under pressure.

2. Adaptability & Flexibility

The single highest-rated competency in the study — still top 3 even when respondents were forced to choose.

3. Collaboration & Teamwork

The competency employers and alumni converge on most strongly.

4. Verbal Communication

Never drops out of the top tier, no matter how the question was asked.

5. Curiosity

Consistently valued, and holds up even under forced trade-offs.

6. Leadership & Professionalism

Undervalued in the abstract — but the first thing people reach for when it actually counts.
